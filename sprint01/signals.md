## Statista — Dating Services Market

### Signal 1
### Sector entering maturity

**Fact**: Online dating revenue change dropped from 14.9% in 2018 to ~2% by 2027–2030.

**Interpretation**: A typical late-stage S-curve. The industry has entered saturation.

**Source**: Statista — Dating Services market data & analysis (Feb 2023)
**Corresponding chart**: Chart 04 — online-dating revenue change
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/statista/online_dating_market_size.md` → Page 2 / Chart 04

---

### Signal 2
### COVID general slowdown ≠ structural reshuffling

**Fact**: M and CD growth rates dropped sharply in 2020; OD dropped sharply in 2021. However, all three subsectors continued to grow in absolute terms, with share migration of only 4–7pp over 13 years.

**Interpretation**: COVID was a general slowdown event, not a structural reshuffling. All three subsectors grew; OD only took a small share from M.

**Source**: Statista — Dating Services market data & analysis (Feb 2023)
**Corresponding chart**: Chart 01 — dating-services revenue (stacked column + line)
**Secondary evidence**: Charts 03 / 06 / 07 — three revenue lines compared
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/statista/online_dating_market_size.md` → Page 1 / Chart 01

---

### Signal 3
### ARPU declining across all three subsectors, M dropping fastest

**Fact**: M's ARPU is approximately $30 higher than OD/CD, but M shows the steepest decline of the three; OD shows the slowest.

**Interpretation**: The high-priced serious matchmaking market is losing monetization capacity fastest. The sector overall is "de-monetizing."

**Source**: Statista — Dating Services market data & analysis (Feb 2023)
**Corresponding chart**: Chart 02 — dating-services ARPU (line)
**Secondary evidence**: Chart 07 (M revenue line) shows M with slowest growth — combined with the largest ARPU drop, this constitutes a "double bleeding" pattern
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/statista/online_dating_market_size.md` → Page 1 / Chart 02

---

### Signal 4
### OD ARPU continued decline after 2019 rebound

**Fact**: OD ARPU rose from $7.03 in 2019 to $7.96 in 2021, then declined continuously, with two further small drops in 2023 and 2025.

**Interpretation**: User acquisition decoupled from monetization — users are growing but per-user revenue is dropping. Paywall strategies are approaching marginal failure.

**Source**: Statista — Dating Services market data & analysis (Feb 2023)
**Corresponding chart**: Chart 05 — online-dating ARPU (line)
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/statista/online_dating_market_size.md` → Page 2 / Chart 05

---

### Signal 5
### Business model wall window: 2027–2030

**Fact**: OD growth rate locks at 2% from 2027–2030; M drops from 1.5% to 1.4% starting 2029.

**Interpretation**: Statista's own forecast has built the "wall" into the data — 2030 is the natural endpoint of the current commercial model. This is critical for the 2046 scenario: Statista is not forecasting growth, it is forecasting stagnation.

**Source**: Statista — Dating Services market data & analysis (Feb 2023)
**Corresponding chart**: Chart 04 — online-dating revenue change
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/statista/online_dating_market_size.md` → Page 2 / Chart 04

### Signal 6
### Subsector share shifting slowly, not dramatically

**Fact**: From 2017 to 2030, M share dropped from 54.5% to 49.8% (-4.7pp), OD share rose from 31.9% to 39.0% (+7.1pp), CD share dropped from 13.6% to 11.2% (-2.4pp).

**Interpretation**: M is yielding ground to OD, but the migration is small. The narrative "serious matchmaking giving way to casual high-frequency" is directionally correct but weak in magnitude — slow erosion, not structural reversal.

**Source**: Statista — Dating Services market data & analysis (Feb 2023)
**Corresponding chart**: Chart 01 — dating-services revenue (stacked column)
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/statista/online_dating_market_size.md` → Page 1 / Chart 01

## UN World Population Prospects 2024

### Signal 7
### Global median age rising

**Fact**: Global median age 31.10 (2026) → 35.21 (2046), +4.11 years over 20 years.

**Interpretation**: Dating is no longer a one-window pursuit between ages 20–35. The industry's economic model, optimized for that narrow window, faces structural mismatch with a population whose dating behavior unfolds over decades, not years.

**Source**: UN DESA Population Division. (2024). *World Population Prospects 2024*. United Nations.
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/UN_WPP/unpopulation_dataportal_20260508163331_world.csv`

---

### Signal 8
### Old-age dependency ratio surge

**Fact**: Old-age dependency ratio 16.24 (2026) → 24.20 (2046), +49% relative increase in 20 years.

**Interpretation**: Working-age populations face significantly greater time and economic pressure across longer lifespans. This compounds with extended life expectancy — what Monica described in class as "four relationships in a 120-year lifespan" approaches lived reality. The industry's user base does not simply shrink with declining birth rates; it stratifies across multiple distinct relationship phases over a 60-to-80-year adult life.

**Source**: UN DESA Population Division. (2024). *World Population Prospects 2024*. United Nations.
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/UN_WPP/unpopulation_dataportal_20260508163331_world.csv`

## IPCC AR6 Synthesis Report SPM

### Signal 9
### Five SSP scenarios = research-grounded cone of plausibility

**Fact**: Warming by 2081–2100 ranges from 1.4°C (SSP1-1.9, very low emissions) to 4.4°C (SSP5-8.5, very high emissions) across five scenarios. SPM Box 1 Table 1 maps these to mitigation pathway categories C1 through C8.

**Interpretation**: Climate change does not give one future — it gives five. The SSP scenarios themselves constitute a research-grounded cone of plausibility: SSP1-1.9 and SSP1-2.6 represent preferred futures where coordinated mitigation succeeds; SSP2-4.5 represents the projected baseline under current trajectories; SSP3-7.0 and SSP5-8.5 represent preposterous but not implausible futures of mitigation failure. IPCC has effectively pre-drawn the cone framework.

**Source**: IPCC. (2023). *Climate Change 2023: Synthesis Report. Summary for Policymakers*. IPCC, Geneva, Switzerland. Section B.1.1, Box 1 Table 1.
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/ipcc/IPCC_AR6_SYR_SPM.pdf`

---

### Signal 10
### Heat-humidity threshold compresses outdoor social space

**Fact**: Under 2.4–3.1°C warming, dangerous heat-humidity days projected to rise from historical baselines to 150–300 days per year in vulnerable regions (Figure SPM.3b).

**Interpretation**: Dating venues have historically depended on outdoor public space (parks, restaurant terraces, walkability). When a third of the year carries mortality risk outdoors in major regions, indoor and mediated dating shifts from preference to structural necessity. "Spring date" becomes a regionally archaic phrase.

**Source**: IPCC. (2023). *Climate Change 2023: Synthesis Report. Summary for Policymakers*. Figure SPM.3, Section B.2.1.
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/ipcc/IPCC_AR6_SYR_SPM.pdf`

---

### Signal 11
### Climate-driven displacement undermines geographic stability

**Fact**: Approximately 3.3–3.6 billion people live in highly vulnerable contexts. Climate and extreme weather events already cause displacement in Africa, Asia, North America, Central and South America (high confidence).

**Interpretation**: Long-term relationships have historically presumed geographic stability — same city, same community. As climate-driven migration shifts from exception to baseline, "same-city" assumptions dissolve. Long-distance, temporary, and cross-regional relationship structures will move from edge case to dominant pattern.

**Source**: IPCC. (2023). *Climate Change 2023: Synthesis Report. Summary for Policymakers*. Section A.2.5, B.2.4.
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/ipcc/IPCC_AR6_SYR_SPM.pdf`

## UN World Urbanization Prospects 2018

### Signal 12
### Global urban share rises to 68% by 2050

**Fact**: Urban share of global population 55% (2018) → 68% (2050). 2.5 billion new urban residents projected, with 90% of growth concentrated in Asia and Africa.

**Interpretation**: The dating industry's user base does not shrink — it migrates. Future industry growth lies in megacities of the Global South, not the saturated Western markets that currently dominate the industry's product logic. The "loneliness paradox" — high physical density coupled with weak social bonds — intensifies as urbanization deepens.

**Source**: UN DESA Population Division. (2018). *World Urbanization Prospects: The 2018 Revision*. United Nations.
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/UN_WUP/WUP2018-KeyFacts.pdf`

## EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689)

### Signal 14
### Article 50 transparency tier creates legal floor for AI-mediated intimacy

**Fact**: Article 50(1) requires AI systems interacting with natural persons to disclose AI status; Article 50(3) requires emotion recognition and biometric categorisation systems to disclose operation; Article 50(2) requires AI-generated synthetic content to be machine-readable as artificial.

**Interpretation**: The "AI partner indistinguishable from a human" product category becomes legally untenable in EU jurisdiction. AI companion products must be designed around disclosed artificiality from the start, not as a retrofit. This redraws what is technologically and commercially viable.

**Source**: European Union. (2024). *Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 13 June 2024 laying down harmonised rules on artificial intelligence (Artificial Intelligence Act)*. Article 50.
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/EU_AI_Act/`

---

### Signal 15
### Article 5(1)(b) "effect" standard exposes engagement algorithms

**Fact**: Article 5(1)(b) prohibits AI exploiting vulnerabilities based on age, disability, or socio-economic situation — applies whether the distortion is intentional OR merely the *effect* of the system's design.

**Interpretation**: This is the law's sharpest cut for the dating industry. Dating algorithms profiting from user loneliness, FOMO, or attachment anxiety are legally exposed even without manipulative design intent. The "we didn't mean to" defense is removed. Algorithm design for engagement metrics now carries legal liability if the *effect* exploits vulnerable users.

**Source**: European Union. (2024). *Regulation (EU) 2024/1689*. Article 5(1)(b).
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/EU_AI_Act/`

---

### Signal 16
### Brussels Effect propagates EU standards globally

**Fact**: EU AI Act enters full implementation August 2026. Historical pattern (GDPR 2018, REACH 2007) shows companies adopting EU-compliant designs as default to access the EU market, propagating EU standards globally.

**Interpretation**: EU regulation is not just regional — through the Brussels Effect, it becomes the de facto global floor for AI in intimate domains. This single variable separates the preferred future (Brussels Effect holds, standards propagate to 38+ countries) from the projected future (regulation fragments, EU stays an island).

**Source**: Bradford, A. (2020). *The Brussels Effect: How the European Union Rules the World*. Oxford University Press. Combined with Regulation (EU) 2024/1689.
**Corresponding file**: `raw_capture/EU_AI_Act/`
